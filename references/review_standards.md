# Academic Paper Review Standards

This document compiles evaluation criteria from Chinese academic writing guides, including common rejection reasons, academic expression guidelines, and format requirements.

---

## Part 1: Common Rejection Reasons

### 1.1 Writing and Format Issues

Format quality significantly impacts first impressions and can sway final judgments. Poor writing quality and logical confusion hinder the reading experience and increase difficulty for reviewers to assess the paper's core value.

**Critical format issues:**
- Chart quality: Monotonous styles, messy formatting, low resolution, excessive numbers
- Language errors: Grammar mistakes, typos, confusing expression
- Punctuation misuse: Especially the confusion of 的/地/得
- Reference format inconsistencies: Incorrect capitalization, missing page numbers, mixed formats

**Statistics-related errors:**
- p-value written as "p=.000" or "p<.000" (incorrect; should be p<.001)
- Missing confidence intervals
- Inadequate reporting of effect sizes

### 1.2 Problem Importance

The most important factor is the paper's contribution. For economics papers:
- Mere model derivation and regression have limited value
- Important economic meaning is essential (solving important theoretical problems, revealing mechanisms)
- Advanced mathematical tools are embellishments, not contributions

**Signs of weak contribution:**
- Unclear problem definition
- Key concepts not properly defined
- Literature review errors or outdated references (e.g., 2022 paper citing only pre-2017 sources)
- Insufficient research motivation (feels like "making dumplings for the vinegar")

### 1.3 Methodological Rigor

**Common methodological problems:**
- Inappropriate method for the research question
- Poor data quality or insufficient sample size
- Models showing consistent underfitting
- Applying methods from other fields without domain adaptation
- Missing validation on independent datasets

**Good practices:**
- Methods must match research question (random assignment for interventions, observational design for traits)
- Acknowledge method limitations
- Explain why chosen methods are appropriate

### 1.4 Research Innovation

**What makes a paper valuable:**
- New technical route or breaking conventional thinking
- Solving real problems (even without strong theory)
- Non-obvious, reproducible results
- Clear advancement over existing work

**Signs of non-valuable papers:**
- Half the paper repeats established theory
- Own work described briefly
- Results guessed from basic theory
- "Technically correct but no scientific value"

### 1.5 Other Issues

- Disrespectful response to reviewers
- Fundamental technical errors (e.g., results orders of magnitude off)
- Ethical concerns
- Poor peer responses

## Part 2: Academic Expression Guidelines

### 2.1 Paper Structure

**Core contribution principle:**
- State the most critical contribution in a separate, focused paragraph
- Explain what the core finding specifically is
- Don't write "we analyzed data on X and found many interesting results"

**Newspaper-style organization:**
- Start with the most important content
- Then explain background and details
- NOT like a "joke" or "mystery novel" that reveals at the end
- Readers are busy - most only care about your main finding

### 2.2 Abstract Guidelines

**Length and content:**
- Limit to 100-150 words (English) or 800 characters (Chinese)
- Convey the core, novel contribution
- Do NOT cite other literature
- Report main findings, NOT research process
- No detailed data analysis or theoretical derivation

**Structure:**
- 1-2 sentences on core research question
- If needed, mention研究对象和样本范围
- Then focus on conclusions: "其一...；其二...；其三..."

**Avoid:**
- Long background introductions (these belong in Introduction)
- Vague statements like "interesting results"

### 2.3 Introduction Guidelines

**Purpose:** Express what you did and main contribution, with explanation

**First sentence guidelines:**
- DON'T start with philosophy ("economists have long suspected...")
- DON'T start with "literature has been interested in X"
- Start with your paper's core contribution itself

**What to avoid:**
- "Road map" paragraph wasting space ("Section 2 sets up model...")
- Starting with 1.5 pages of literature review
- Stating only conclusions without facts
- Writing "I will study XX in the future" - readers don't care

**Recommended structure:**
1. Background and motivation (1-2 paragraphs)
2. Clear contribution statement with "The contribution of this paper is..."
3. Brief model description (1-2 paragraphs)
4. Main results overview (1-2 pages)
5. Relationship to existing literature (1-2 pages)

### 2.4 Literature Review Guidelines

**Purpose:** Organize existing research into coherent picture to identify your research gap

**Common mistakes:**
- Simple chronological listing of papers
- Literature摘抄 rather than synthesis
- Too many or too few references

**Good practices:**
- Organize by theme/logic, not chronology
- Compare 2-3 closest papers specifically
- Identify the "missing piece" in the puzzle
- Focus on recent work (past 5 years) with classic foundations
- Cite top journal papers in your field
- Balance between being comprehensive and focused

### 2.5 Methodology Section

**Theory:**
- Should support understanding empirical work
- Don't write a "general" model - list only what you need
- Model helps understand results, that's its purpose

**Empirical results:**
- Start with main results, not preliminary exploration
- Present intuitive charts for main findings
- Limited presentation of robustness checks

### 2.6 Conclusion Guidelines

**Purpose:** Brief and targeted

**What to include:**
- Brief 2-3 sentence回顾 of core content
- 3-5 specific conclusions using "其一...；其二...；其三..."
- 1-2 paragraphs acknowledging limitations

**What to avoid:**
- Repeating all findings
- Excessive length (1-2 pages maximum)
- Generic policy recommendations not based on results

### 2.7 Writing Style

**Be concise:**
- Every word should have value
- Ask: "Can I say this with fewer words?"
- Don't repeat - if said once, don't say again

**Follow this sequence:**
1. Describe what you did
2. Explain why
3. Compare with alternatives
4. Compare with other scholars' methods

**Pursue precision:**
- Review every sentence for meaning and accuracy
- Ensure reproducibility by peer graduate students

**Simple is beautiful:**
- Don't make things seem complex and impressive
- Simpler math is better
- Clearer estimation techniques preferred

**Avoid:**
- Footnotes for inline comments (put in text if important, delete if not)
- Adjectives describing your work ("amazing results," "very important")
- Double adjectives if any used
- Long fancy words when simple ones work

### 2.8 Tables and Figures

**Table requirements:**
- Each table needs an independent title
- Use three-line tables (三线表) for academic papers
- Correct significant figures (2-3 effective digits)
- Reasonable units (percentages often clearer)
- Cannot span pages
- Consistent font size throughout

**Figure requirements:**
- Good figures bring papers to life
- Better than large tables for showing patterns
- Each figure needs independent title
- Mark axes, use reasonable units
- Don't use invisible line types
- Consistent fonts with text

**Table/Figure titles:**
- Figure titles below figures
- Table titles above tables
- Sequential numbering: 图3-1, 表3-1
- When referring: "结果参见图3-1" not "参见上图"

### 2.9 Mathematical Formulas

- Use MathType software
- Sequential numbering (right-aligned)
- Number format: (章序号-公式序号) e.g., (4-1)
- Formula font size should match body text
- Variable fonts must match text
- Define all Greek letters clearly in accessible location

### 2.10 References

**Quantity and quality:**
- MBA: 20+ references, 5+ English
- Finance master: Double the above
- Quality over quantity - focus on relevance and journal quality
- Prioritize top 5 journals in your field

**Composition:**
- Mainly journal articles
- Some classic books
- Network sources must have full URL

**Balance:**
- Recent (past 5 years) as primary
- Early classics for foundational theories

**Formatting:**
- In-text citations must match reference list
- Consistent format throughout
- Complete information (authors, title, journal, volume, issue, pages)

## Part 3: Research Contribution Evaluation

### 3.1 What Constitutes Contribution?

**For theoretical model papers:**
1. Proposing a new economic question
2. Providing a new model/perspective on a problem
3. Solving an important application problem
4. Revealing key economic factors
5. Generating new empirical predictions
6. Making technical contributions (new proof methods, computational techniques)

**For empirical papers:**
1. Identifying new causal relationships
2. Providing new evidence on existing questions
3. Using superior methods to address questions
4. Revealing mechanisms behind relationships
5. Demonstrating practical significance

### 3.2 Contribution Statement Guidelines

**DO:**
- Be specific about what's new
- Compare directly with closest existing work
- Explain why the difference matters
- Use "The contribution of this paper is..." format

**DON'T:**
- Make vague claims about "filling gaps"
- Overly criticize others (reviewers may be authors)
- Claim contributions without clear differentiation
- Use inflated language ("groundbreaking," "revolutionary")

### 3.3 Assessing Contribution Magnitude

**Strong contributions:**
- Change how people think about a problem
- Enable new types of analysis
- Solve long-standing puzzles
- Have wide applicability

**Moderate contributions:**
- Add nuance to existing findings
- Replicate in new context
- Combine existing approaches

**Weak contributions:**
- Obvious extensions
- Replication without new insight
- Method over substance

## Part 4: Format Requirements Checklist

### 4.1 Overall Format

- [ ] Font: 小四号 (12pt) body text, 宋体 for Chinese, Times New Roman for English
- [ ] Line spacing: 1.2x for body text
- [ ] Margins as per school/journal requirements
- [ ] Page numbering consistent

### 4.2 Section Structure

- [ ] Chapter headings: Level 1 (第一章)
- [ ] Section headings: Level 2 (2.1 xxx)
- [ ] Subsection headings: Level 3 (2.1.1 xxx)
- [ ] No Level 4 - use 1. xxx; 2. xxx or (1) xxx; (2) xxx instead

### 4.3 Per-Chapter Requirements

Each chapter:
- [ ] Opening paragraph: Introduction to chapter purpose and content (3-7 lines)
- [ ] Closing section: "X.X 本章小结" (Chapter Summary)

### 4.4 Citation Format

- [ ] Consistent citation style throughout (APA, MLA, GB/T 7714, etc.)
- [ ] All in-text citations have corresponding references
- [ ] All references are cited in text
- [ ] Proper p-value notation (p<.001, not p=.000)

### 4.5 Table Format

- [ ] Three-line table format (三线表)
- [ ] No cross-page tables
- [ ] Consistent font size (五号 or 小五号 for dense tables)
- [ ] Self-explanatory (80% understandable without text)
- [ ] Source note at bottom
- [ ] Variable/indicator explanations at bottom

## Part 5: Research Quality Indicators

### 5.1 Methodological Quality

**Strong methodology:**
- Clear identification strategy
- Appropriate research design
- Adequate sample size with power analysis
- Proper control variables
- Robustness checks
- Acknowledgment of limitations

**Weak methodology:**
- Insufficient justification for method choice
- Inadequate sample size
- Missing key controls
- No robustness checks
- Overclaiming results

### 5.2 Data Quality

**High-quality data:**
- Clear data source
- Appropriate time period
- Representative sample
- Reliable measures
- Sufficient observations

**Problematic data:**
- Unclear sources
- Inappropriate time frame
- Selection bias
- Measurement issues
- Too few observations

### 5.3 Analysis Quality

**Good analysis:**
- Economic significance explained, not just statistical
- Effect sizes reported
- Confidence intervals provided
- Results interpreted in context
- Multiple specifications tested

**Poor analysis:**
- Only p-values reported
- No discussion of magnitude
- Overinterpretation of weak results
- Cherry-picked specifications
- Results not connected to theory

## Part 6: Common Academic Writing Problems

### 6.1 Abstract Issues

- Too much background, too little results
- Vague statements instead of specific findings
- Including citations
- Describing process rather than results
- Missing key conclusion

### 6.2 Introduction Issues

- Starts too broad/philosophical
- Buries lead finding
- Missing research question
- Inadequate motivation
- No clear contribution statement
- Literature review too long

### 6.3 Literature Review Issues

- Simple listing without synthesis
- Important papers omitted
- Over-reliance on old sources
- Not identifying clear gap
- Not organizing by theme

### 6.4 Conclusion Issues

- Simply repeating results
- Too long
- Generic policy recommendations
- Not acknowledging limitations
- New ideas introduced without support

### 6.5 Writing Style Issues

- Excessive use of passive voice
- Nouns created from verbs (nominalization)
- Overly complex sentence structure
- Repeating points across sections
- Using fancy words when simple ones work
- Inconsistent terminology
